GLOSSARY

Key terms and definitions

Reference definitions for the concepts, benchmarks, and acronyms used throughout this research.

HLMI

High-Level Machine Intelligence

The point at which unaided machines can accomplish every task better and more cheaply than humans. Used as the standard metric in AI researcher surveys by Grace et al. HLMI is the most widely cited benchmark in forecasting literature — median predictions have compressed from 2060 (2022 survey) to 2040 (2023 survey).

FAOL

Full Automation of Labor

Complete replacement of human labor across all occupations. A stricter benchmark than HLMI — the Grace et al. surveys show FAOL predictions running decades behind HLMI. Where HLMI asks "can machines do it?", FAOL asks "have machines actually replaced all human workers?" The gap between the two reflects implementation friction, regulation, and societal inertia.

AGI

Artificial General Intelligence

AI that can perform any intellectual task a human can. Definitions vary widely — from "economically valuable autonomous work" (OpenAI) to "any work a man can do" (classical definition). The lack of a consensus definition makes AGI timelines difficult to compare across sources. This site uses HLMI and FAOL as more precisely defined alternatives where possible.

Agentic AI

Agentic AI

AI systems that autonomously plan, execute multi-step tasks, and make decisions without continuous human guidance. Distinct from assistive AI (autocomplete, chat). Examples include Claude Code, Devin, and Cursor agent mode. Agentic adoption is growing rapidly but remains the minority mode — roughly 21.5% of AI coding tool usage as of early 2025.

Assistive AI

Assistive AI

AI tools that augment human work in real-time — code completion, chat-based Q&A, inline suggestions. The dominant mode of AI coding tool usage (~78.5% adoption). Assistive AI keeps the developer in the loop at all times, in contrast to agentic AI which operates autonomously on tasks.

Verification Debt

Verification Debt

The hidden cost of AI-generated code: developers spend less time writing and more time reviewing, debugging, and verifying AI output. DORA 2025 found AI hurts delivery stability while boosting throughput — teams ship faster but break more. 45% of developers report longer debugging time with AI-generated code (Stack Overflow 2025).

ACI

Artificial Capable Intelligence

Coined by Mustafa Suleyman as a more practical goalpost than AGI. An AI that can autonomously perform economically valuable tasks — not necessarily all tasks, but enough to function as an independent economic agent. ACI sidesteps the definitional debates around AGI by focusing on measurable economic output.

Time Horizon (METR)

Time Horizon (METR)

The "50% time horizon" — the task duration at which frontier AI agents succeed half the time. Has been doubling every ~89 days since 2024. METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) tracks this metric as a proxy for how complex a task AI can reliably handle. A longer time horizon means agents can tackle more ambitious, multi-step work.

SWE-bench

SWE-bench

A benchmark measuring AI ability to resolve real GitHub issues. Used to track frontier model capability for software engineering tasks. Scores have risen from ~4% (early 2024) to 72% on the verified subset (early 2025), reflecting rapid gains in AI coding ability. Created by Princeton researchers as a more realistic alternative to synthetic coding benchmarks.
